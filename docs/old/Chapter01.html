Chapter 01: Natural Deduction

ProofTree is two different systems that work together. First, it is a formal syntax for writing proofs of mathematical statements. Second, it is a program that verifies that proofs written in this syntax are valid. These two systems work together to make up ProofTree: users write proofs using the ProofTree syntax, and then the ProofTree program confirms that these proofs are valid. This guide serves to enable you to write proofs using the ProofTree syntax, while also cultivating an understanding of how the ProofTree program confirms that validates your proofs.

To start, open a new file (Notebook is fine, but you can download and use SublimeText if you want to be fancy) and name it Main.proof. This is where you will write your ProofTree proofs, and where the ProofTree program will look to verify your proofs.

ProofTree proofs are centered around the idea of a proposition. A proposition is simply a precise mathematical statement -- it need not be true. For example, "every integer is either odd or even" is a proposition, as is "every real number is greater than 7." The statement, "x = 7" is not a proposition, unless we have previoisly specificed what x is refering to (for example, "if x is an integer greater than 8 but less than 6, then x = 7" is a valid proposition).

Without any direction, the ProofTree system starts with no information about the mathematical universe we reside in. It has no notion of numbers, sets, functions, or anything else. In some ways, this imbues it with great power, as we can use ProofTree to develop any mathematical system we might desire. However, it also means that we have to put in the work to devleop these systems.

Let's start by creating a new predicate. A predicate is a function that maps a number of objects to a proposition. For example, the equals sign = is a predicate that maps two objects a and b to the proposition a = b. You could also have a predicate that maps zero objects to a proposition. For example, True and False are such predicates, that map to the propositions of the same name. We'll create a new predicate with the name P.

opqaue predicate P

The opqaue identifier simply means that we don't yet know anything about the predicate P.

Now we will prove a very simple theorem about P: P -> P (that is, if we know P is True, then we know P is True).

opqaue predicate P theorem p_imples_p : P -> P { P | assumption }

The theorem identifier indicates that what follows is the decleration and proof of a new theorem. We first indicate the name of the theorem (p_implies_p), followed by the colon, and then finally by the proposition that we are trying to prove (P -> P). We then use an opening curly brace to indicate that our proof is beginning, and will use a closing curly brace once our proof is complete.

The most common way to prove an implication (that is, a theorem of the form A -> B, or in our case P -> P) is to assume that the first half (the antecedent) is True, and then use this information to prove the second half of the implication (the consequent). This is a process known as natural deduction. In order to carry out a natural deduction proof in ProofTree, we need to understand the concept of the "goal" of a proof.

Every ProofTree proof has a goal. When you begin the proof, the goal is just the proposition listed in the theorem decleration. However, the goal might change over the course of the proof. For example, in a natural deduction proof, the goal would start as an implication (let's say, A -> B). After we assume that A is True, the goal becomes just to prove B. You don't need to indicate the new goal anywhere in your proof -- the ProofTree program will automatically recognize that you are carrying our natural deduction and will change the goal accordingly. At the end of the proof, we just need to make sure that the goal is True.

We carry out natural deduction in our proof with the following line:

P | assumption

The assumption keyword (called a justification) means "assume that P is True according to natural deduction, and change the goal accordingly." Thus, the ProofTree program will change the goal of the proof from P -> P to just P.

But we just assumed that P was True! This means that our proof is complete, and we end our proof with a closing curly brace.

To confirm that our proof is valid, we run the ProofTree program. You should receive the following message (your speeds may very):

Complied Main.proof Compilied at 64 milliseconds Proof of p_imples_p is valid! Completed at 69 milliseconds

Congrats! You have proven your first theorem in ProofTree. In the ensuing chapters you will learn about other proof techniques, how to use theorems you've already proved, how to talk about numbers, and more. Welcome to ProofTree!
